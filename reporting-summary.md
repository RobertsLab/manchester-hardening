# Oyster Thermal Hardening Experiment: Grant Reporting Summary

## Executive Summary

This document summarizes a thermal hardening (priming) experiment conducted at Manchester Research Station in spring-summer 2025 to evaluate whether heat conditioning improves field performance of Pacific oysters (*Crassostrea gigas*). The experiment involved exposing juvenile oysters to elevated temperatures during a controlled conditioning phase, followed by field deployment to assess survival and growth outcomes.

## Experimental Overview

**Location:** Manchester Research Station, Washington  
**Timeline:** May 30 - September 23, 2025 (115 days total)  
**Species:** Pacific oyster (*Crassostrea gigas*)  
**Total Oysters:** 879 starting oysters from 10 genetic families  
**Experimental Design:** Two-treatment comparison (High temperature vs. Control) with field deployment

- Mean volume: 1,090 ± 953 mm³ (SD)
- Size range: 5.85 - 8,583 mm³ (volume)
- Age: Approximately 1 year old (2024 cohort measured in 2025)

**Sample Sizes:**
- Control treatment: 440 oysters across 20 family bags
- High temperature treatment: 439 oysters across 20 family bags
- Replication: Each family represented in both treatments (2 replicates per treatment)
- Total of 40 tagged family bags

### Experimental Design

**Tank Configuration:**
- 4 experimental tanks total
- Tanks 1 and 2: High temperature treatment
- Tanks 3 and 4: Control treatment
- Each tank contained 10 family bags (one replicate of each family)

**Cage Assignments:**
- Tank 1 (High): Cage B028
- Tank 2 (High): Cage R013
- Tank 3 (Control): Cage R008
- Tank 4 (Control): Cage G058

**Environmental Monitoring:**
- Temperature logged continuously using 4 Hobo Tidbit loggers (one per tank)
- Logger serial numbers: 22105353, 22105354, 22105356, 22105358
- Recording interval: 10 minutes

### Thermal Conditioning/Priming Procedure

**Conditioning Phase Duration:** May 30 - June 16, 2025 (approximately 17 days)

**Temperature Treatments:**

*High Temperature Treatment (Tanks 1 and 2):*
- Elevated temperature exposure designed to induce thermal hardening
- Peak temperatures reached approximately 24-26°C
- Temperature fluctuations created through controlled heating

*Control Treatment (Tanks 3 and 4):*
- Ambient Manchester seawater conditions
- Temperatures maintained at approximately 12-16°C
- Minimal temperature variation

**Procedure for Reproduction:**

1. **Setup:**
   - Obtain juvenile oysters (~1 year old, 15-25 mm length)
   - Organize into family groups in mesh bags (10-45 oysters per bag depending on family size)
   - Tag each bag with unique identifier
   - Distribute families evenly across treatment tanks (2 bags per family, 1 per treatment tank)

2. **Temperature Conditioning:**
   - Deploy temperature loggers in each tank
   - For high treatment tanks: Gradually increase temperature to 24-26°C range
   - For control tanks: Maintain ambient seawater flow-through (~12-16°C)
   - Maintain consistent water flow and aeration
   - Duration: Approximately 2-3 weeks

3. **Monitoring:**
   - Record temperatures every 10 minutes using data loggers
   - Monitor oyster behavior and mortality daily
   - Maintain water quality (flow, aeration, cleaning as needed)

4. **Field Deployment:**
   - After conditioning period, transfer all oysters to field cages
   - Deploy cages at Manchester Research Station field site
   - Continue temperature monitoring in field conditions
   - Duration: Approximately 14-16 weeks

### Field Deployment Phase

**Duration:** June 16 - September 23, 2025 (approximately 99 days)

**Field Conditions:**
- Location: Manchester Research Station field site
- Oysters deployed in 4 field cages (maintaining cage assignments from tank phase)
- Natural seawater temperature range: 10-20°C
- Temperature monitoring continued throughout field phase

### Assessment Methods

**Size Measurements:**
- Initial measurements: May 30, 2025 (n=840 oysters measured)
- Final measurements: September 23, 2025 (n=827 oysters measured)
- Methodology: Image analysis
  - Photographs taken of each oyster
  - Digital measurements of length (longest shell dimension) and width (perpendicular to length)
  - Conversion ratio calibrated per image (pixels to mm)
  - Volume calculated as ellipsoid: Volume = k × length × width², where k = 0.21

**Mortality Assessment:**
- Final count: September 23, 2025
- All oysters in each family bag counted as live or dead
- Individual mortality recorded per bag (n=41 bags assessed)

## Results

### Mortality Outcomes

**Overall Mortality:**
- Control treatment: 12 dead / 508 total = 2.36% mortality
- High treatment: 17 dead / 469 total = 3.62% mortality

**Mortality by Treatment:**
- Mean mortality Control: 2.34 ± 3.32% (SD)
- Mean mortality High: 3.04 ± 3.77% (SD)

**Statistical Analysis:**
- ANOVA test: No significant effect of treatment on mortality (p > 0.05)
- Mixed effects model accounting for cage and tank effects: No significant treatment effect
- Family effects: Some variation observed among families (see table above)

**Summary:** Thermal hardening treatment did not significantly affect mortality rates. Overall survival was high (>96%) across both treatments.

### Growth and Size Outcomes

**Final Size (September 23, 2025):**
- Overall mean length: 47.25 ± 12.27 mm (SD)
- Overall mean width: 31.65 ± 8.36 mm (SD)
- Overall mean volume: 11,772 ± 8,560 mm³ (SD)
- Size range: 371 - 65,026 mm³ (volume)

**Final Size by Treatment:**
- Control treatment: 11,381 ± 8,209 mm³ (n=416 oysters)
- High treatment: 12,169 ± 8,894 mm³ (n=411 oysters)

**Growth (Change from Initial to Final):**
- Overall volume increase: ~10-fold increase over 115 days
- From ~1,090 mm³ to ~11,772 mm³ average volume

**Statistical Analysis of Size:**

*Final Size Analysis (excluding family YC24-099 due to missing initial data):*
- Mixed effects model: `volume ~ family × treatment + (1|tag) + (1|cage)`
- Family effect: Significant (p < 0.001)
- Treatment effect: Not significant (p = 0.348)
- Family × Treatment interaction: Not significant (p = 0.139)

*Growth Rate Analysis (with time as factor):*
- Mixed effects model: `volume ~ date × family × treatment + (1|tag) + (1|cage)`
- Type III ANOVA results:
  - Date effect: Highly significant (p < 2.2e-16) - oysters grew significantly over time
  - Family effect: Significant (p = 0.0005) - genetic variation in growth
  - Treatment effect: Not significant (p = 0.348)
  - Date × Treatment interaction: Significant (p = 0.005) - treatment affected growth trajectory
  - Date × Family × Treatment interaction: Significant (p = 1.5e-12) - complex interactive effects

**Summary:** While thermal hardening did not significantly affect final size, there were significant interactive effects between time, family, and treatment on growth trajectories. Some families showed differential growth responses to the thermal conditioning treatment over time.

### Key Findings

1. **Survival:** High survival (>96%) in both control and heat-conditioned groups. No significant mortality differences between treatments.

2. **Growth:** Robust growth observed in all groups (~10-fold volume increase over 115 days). Final sizes did not differ significantly between treatments overall.

3. **Family Effects:** Significant genetic variation (family effects) observed for both mortality and growth traits, indicating the importance of family-level variation in these experiments.

4. **Interactive Effects:** Significant date × treatment and date × family × treatment interactions suggest that the thermal conditioning had context-dependent effects on growth patterns that varied by genetic family and time.

5. **Field Performance:** Both control and heat-conditioned oysters performed well in field conditions following the conditioning period, with low mortality and strong growth.

## Conclusions

This thermal hardening experiment at Manchester Research Station evaluated whether short-term (17-day) exposure to elevated temperatures (~24-26°C) could improve subsequent field performance of juvenile Pacific oysters. The results indicate:

- **No clear benefit** of thermal hardening for overall survival or final size under the specific conditions tested
- **High baseline performance** in both groups suggests good overall oyster health and favorable field conditions
- **Significant genetic variation** among families indicates potential for selective breeding
- **Complex growth dynamics** with treatment effects varying by family and time, suggesting that thermal conditioning may have subtle effects that depend on genetic background

These findings contribute to understanding thermal conditioning approaches for shellfish aquaculture and suggest that while thermal hardening did not provide universal benefits in this trial, family-specific responses warrant further investigation. Future work could explore:
- Different conditioning temperature regimes or durations
- Testing under more stressful field conditions
- Focusing on families that showed positive responses to conditioning
- Examining additional performance metrics (e.g., disease resistance, reproductive output)
